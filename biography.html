Biography

The life story of William Bradley "Brad" Pitt

Early Life

William Bradley "Brad" Pitt was born on December 18, 1963, in Shawnee, Oklahoma, to William Alvin Pitt, a trucking company proprietor, and Jane Etta (n√©e Hillhouse), a school counselor. He and his family moved away when he was a baby, and he grew up in Springfield, Missouri.

Brad was raised in a conservative household and had a typical Midwestern upbringing. He was involved in sports, debates, and student government during his school years. He showed early interest in acting and participated in school plays and musicals.

Education

Pitt attended the University of Missouri, where he majored in journalism with a focus on advertising. He was a member of the Sigma Chi fraternity and involved in various campus activities. However, just shy of graduation in the mid-1980s, he made a life-changing decision to leave for California to pursue his dream of becoming an actor.

This bold move represented a significant turning point in his life, as he left behind the security of his Missouri roots to chase his Hollywood dreams.

Career Beginnings

After moving to Los Angeles, Pitt took acting lessons and worked various jobs to support himself, including driving strippers to parties, moving refrigerators, and dressing as a chicken for the restaurant chain El Pollo Loco.

His first acting roles were small parts in television shows and B-movies in the late 1980s. He appeared in shows like "Dallas," "21 Jump Street," and "Growing Pains." These early experiences helped him hone his craft and build connections in the industry.

Breakthrough: Thelma & Louise (1991)

Brad Pitt's career breakthrough came with his role as the charming cowboy hitchhiker J.D. in Ridley Scott's road film "Thelma & Louise" (1991). Though he had only a supporting role, his charismatic performance and memorable scenes, particularly the iconic shirtless robbery scene, made him an overnight sensation and established him as a rising star in Hollywood.

This role showcased his natural screen presence and marked the beginning of his journey to becoming one of Hollywood's most sought-after leading men.

Rise to Stardom

Following "Thelma & Louise," Pitt took on leading roles in several acclaimed films that cemented his status as a major movie star:

  • A River Runs Through It (1992) - Directed by Robert Redford, this film showcased his dramatic acting abilities
  • Legends of the Fall (1994) - His role as Tristan Ludlow earned him widespread recognition and a large female fanbase
  • Interview with the Vampire (1994) - Starring alongside Tom Cruise, this gothic horror film demonstrated his versatility
  • Seven (1995) - This psychological thriller with Morgan Freeman proved his ability to handle dark, complex material
  • Fight Club (1999) - Perhaps one of his most iconic roles as Tyler Durden, showcasing his range as an actor

Evolution as an Actor and Producer

Throughout the 2000s, Pitt continued to evolve both as an actor and as a producer. He found commercial success with Steven Soderbergh's "Ocean's" trilogy, starring alongside George Clooney and Matt Damon. These films not only were box office hits but also established lifelong friendships and collaborations.

In 2001, he co-founded Plan B Entertainment, initially with his then-wife Jennifer Aniston and producer Brad Grey. This production company would go on to produce numerous critically acclaimed and socially conscious films, demonstrating Pitt's commitment to meaningful storytelling beyond just acting.

Current Status

Today, Brad Pitt is recognized as one of Hollywood's most influential figures, with a career spanning over 30 years. His films have grossed over $9.3 billion worldwide, and he has an estimated net worth of $400 million as of 2024. He continues to act in and produce films, with recent projects including "F1" (2025) and "Wolfs" (2024) with George Clooney.

Beyond his entertainment career, Pitt is known for his humanitarian efforts and advocacy for various causes, including disaster relief, environmental issues, and LGBTQ+ rights.
